# Character Inference

## Inference Order

Derive the character in this order:

1. Establish cultural background, age range, wealth, a real specific occupation, and social authority. Keep the character unnamed unless a name is requested or useful.
2. Verify that the occupation has explainable real responsibilities, work environments, common tools, and occupational clothing; replace vague conceptual titles.
3. Define the occupation's concrete responsibilities rather than relying on its title.
4. Add two or three behavior habits that could affect clothing or carried objects.
5. Identify at least one relationship, experience, memory, aesthetic preference, or collecting habit that could produce a personal object.
6. Establish climate, scene, immediate goal, movement needs, and likely risks.
7. Determine what identity the character wants others to perceive.
8. Convert those facts into clothing, accessory, carrying, and wearing-relationship requirements.

## Useful Scene Families

- daily life and commuting
- workplace and fieldwork
- business, negotiation, and formal events
- social entertainment, nightlife, and dating
- exercise, competition, and recovery
- travel and transit
- ceremony, celebration, and ritual
- security, combat, and concealed danger
- outdoor exploration and severe weather
- home and private downtime
- disguise, infiltration, and hidden identity

## Derivation Questions

Use these questions internally, then report only concise conclusions:

- What must the character communicate at first glance?
- What movements must remain possible?
- What weather, hazards, or social rules matter?
- What must be carried, accessed quickly, concealed, or protected?
- Which personal object could show a habit, relationship, experience, memory, taste, or collecting behavior?
- What is currently being worn, connected, carried, stored, adjusted, temporarily held, or used?
- Which large object could plausibly affect the silhouette in the current activity?
- How much comfort, mobility, subtlety, or practicality will the character sacrifice for status and appearance?
- Which habit leaves a visible trace on clothing or equipment?

## Guardrails

- Default to a neutral role-based label derived from the selected real occupation during outfit tests instead of inventing a personal name.
- When a name is requested, derive it from the character's cultural and linguistic context. Avoid stock literary-sounding names, repeated surname-name rhythms, and names remembered from previous or unrelated characters.
- Do not use nationality as a costume stereotype. Let it influence details only when culturally and contextually justified.
- Do not equate wealth with excessive decoration. Wealth may appear through fit, material, restraint, maintenance, or custom construction.
- Do not equate a minimal or monochrome outfit with a need for a large gem, ornate brooch, luminous core, or other conspicuous focal ornament.
- Prefer lived-in personal and carried objects for ordinary daily characters unless a high-visual-weight object has a credible character, cultural, structural, equipment, or compositional basis.
- Do not make every profession wear a uniform.
- Do not invent vague professional titles whose real duties cannot be explained.
- Avoid labor-intensive occupations by default, but use them when explicitly requested or narratively important.
