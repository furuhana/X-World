# Outfit Slots and Density

## Slot Status

Assign each possible slot one status:

- **Required:** necessary for the scene, action, or identity.
- **Derived candidate:** actively generated from the character, wearing relationships, activity, or composition, then evaluated for selection.
- **Optional:** available when useful, but not required merely because the slot exists.
- **Omitted:** exclude because it adds no value, duplicates another item, creates conflict, or makes the design too busy.

Never output empty slots.

Accessory slots are exploration prompts, not a checklist to fill. Every character must actively derive accessory and carried-object candidates before assigning final slot status.

## Apply the Element Deployment Plan

Select garments and accessories after defining the core visual element and its full-body deployment plan.

For each selected slot, decide:

1. Does this location need an element application, or should it remain quiet?
2. Which planned role does it carry: primary, secondary, accent, or implicit?
3. Which application method suits its construction or function?
4. Does this application repeat a complete motif already shown elsewhere?
5. Does adding it upset the upper-versus-lower-body visual balance?

Prefer varied applications across construction, wearing relationships, silhouette, and restrained ornament. Useful applications may include shaped pockets, reinforcement panels, protective overlays, adjustable tabs, closures, connectors, suspension points, mounts, hardware, cross-body structures, or hanging relationships. Use structural applications on major garments only when they follow plausible construction. Use restrained planar applications at focal accents and small accessory applications only when they add useful rhythm. Do not force every slot to carry the element or default its useful applications to the waist.

Do not cut an element-shaped opening into a single garment layer merely for decoration. Use a layered edge, attached overlay, shaped pocket, reinforced panel, or functional component instead. Allow an unusual opening only when it provides access, ventilation, movement, adjustment, or a secured reveal of an underlayer; specify how its edges and operation are constructed.

Only add functional components that answer an established character need. Avoid decorative pseudo-function and do not default to generic cargo, utility, or tactical styling.

## Clothing Slots

- outer layer
- middle layer
- inner layer
- independent arm garments
- trousers or lower-body base garment
- additional lower-body layer
- socks
- footwear

Use broad outer-layer families as starting points: none, short jacket, long coat, tailored outerwear, uniform outerwear, functional outerwear, protective outerwear, or ceremonial outerwear. Infer the concrete garment and attributes from context.

For the default fighter-scale heavyweight body:

- avoid sharply cinched waists, long narrow cuts, and tailoring that creates a lean V-taper
- allow room through the moderately broad waist, massive thighs, and heavy calves without creating a smooth oversized belly
- when using full-length trousers with low-cut or ankle-height footwear, end the hems slightly above the shoe openings so a narrow band of white socks remains visible while standing
- keep trouser silhouette, leg width, hem opening, cuff construction, and degree of taper free to vary; visible white socks must not imply or require jogger-style or elasticated hems
- prevent trouser hems from stacking on, draping over, or hiding the footwear
- do not force visible socks when the selected footwear naturally covers the ankle, such as taller boots
- keep the main chest-and-abdomen mass readable; outer layers may frame it, while designed necklaces, cross-body straps, harnesses, suspended objects, cape fasteners, and chest connectors may cross or cover limited areas when their wearing relationship is intentional and they do not meaninglessly conceal the whole torso
- use tight inner layers whose stretched fabric clearly traces the pectoral and six-block abdominal forms through tension, shallow valleys, and form shadows
- prevent high collars, raised lapels, scarves, hoods, and massive trapezius forms from enclosing or visually shrinking the head
- keep footwear and accessories scaled to oversized hands, joints, and feet

## Accessory, Carrying, and Wearing-Relationship Derivation

Every character must complete this derivation. Generate at least:

- one personal-object candidate from identity, habit, aesthetic preference, relationship, experience, memory, or collecting behavior
- one object with a wearing, connecting, carrying, storage, adjustment, temporary-holding, or use relationship
- one large-object candidate that may materially change the silhouette

Usually preserve two or three meaningful objects, adjusted for restrained, balanced, or rich design density. This is a loose upper-level guideline, not a minimum count. A complete derivation may retain only one object. Never retain an object merely to satisfy object count, body-region variety, or accessory density.

Derive candidates from any credible combination of:

- occupational needs
- behavior habits
- current activity and environment
- social identity, group relationships, and interpersonal relationships
- personal experience, commemoration, memory, and collecting behavior
- aesthetic preference
- clothing fastening, adjustment, and temporary-storage needs
- carrying tools, weapons, or private belongings
- composition and silhouette needs

An accessory does not need a practical function, but it must have a credible source, wearing method, or clear visual responsibility.

Explore at least three different regions or relationships before selecting final objects:

- **Ear and ear-area wearing:** derive from communication, monitoring, music, hearing protection or assistance, identity expression, aesthetic preference, and connections to eyewear, headwear, hair, or other devices.
- **Neck-and-chest suspension and connection:** derive what exists, what it suspends, fixes, closes, identifies, or connects; establish length, weight, swing range, neckline relationship, torso relationship, and attachment method.
- **Cross-body structures:** derive straps, harnesses, slings, and connectors that distribute weight or connect multiple regions.
- **Shoulder-and-back relationships:** derive shoulder-carried, back-mounted, draped, worn, or suspended objects and their attachment, weight, access, and movement effects.
- **Handheld and arm relationships:** derive held, braced, looped, strapped, protected, or temporarily carried objects.
- **Hip-and-leg wrapping, suspension, and attachment:** derive wrapping, tying, temporary clothing storage, tool or personal-object attachment, hanging, storage, and connections to the chest, shoulder, or leg.
- **External silhouette-changing objects:** derive large handheld objects, long equipment, packs, cases, capes, cloaks, shawls, draped clothing, or multi-region carrying systems.

Exploring a region does not justify selecting it. Final placement must follow the object's weight, shape, use frequency, access path, attachment method, and the character's current action.

The final selection must not default every small object to the waist merely for convenience. However, waist and hip placement is preferred when it is the most credible location for frequently accessed tools, compact tool pouches, and other suitably weighted work objects. Relationship categories must remain open-ended and must not prescribe a specific item such as an earring, chain, belt pouch, or waist tool.

## Object State, Load, and Placement

Assign every selected object exactly one current state:

- **In active use:** visibly operated, read, written on, played, aimed, or otherwise used now.
- **Hand-carried:** held for transport but not actively used.
- **Worn:** fitted closely to its intended body location.
- **Attached for access:** secured to a plausible mount, pouch, strap, holster, or carrying system.
- **Stored:** visibly contained in a pocket, case, bag, or other storage structure.

Do not leave an object's state ambiguous. An occupational object does not need to be visible merely because the character owns or uses it at work.

For every selected object, answer all four placement questions:

1. What supports its weight?
2. What visibly prevents it from slipping, swinging excessively, or falling?
3. How is it accessed or used?
4. Does it obstruct the character's current action, clothing, or another object?

If any answer is unclear, relocate, store, or omit the object.

Apply these location constraints:

- **Wrist:** reserve for lightweight, close-fitting wrist-worn objects such as a watch, narrow bracelet, or fitted wrist device. Do not suspend notebooks, memo pads, tool pouches, bags, or other substantial objects from the wrist.
- **Forearm and upper arm:** reserve for close-fitting protective gear, sleeves, armbands, or purpose-built fitted devices. Do not hang bags, tool kits, books, or loose heavy objects from the arm.
- **Shoulder straps and bag straps:** allow connectors, adjustment hardware, and small purpose-built strap attachments. Do not relocate a normally body-worn functional object, such as a wristwatch, onto a strap unless it is explicitly redesigned as a different strap-mounted device.
- **Waist and hip:** prefer for frequently accessed compact tools, tool rolls, holsters, and suitably weighted pouches when movement and clothing permit.
- **Cross-body, shoulder, and back carrying:** prefer for medium or large bags, document cases, tool cases, and other loads that need distributed support.
- **Hand:** reserve for objects required by the current action or intentionally being transported. Do not fill a hand merely to reinforce occupational identity.

## Hand Occupancy and Bilateral Items

Give each hand at most one clear action. Treat an open expressive, balancing, gesturing, or stabilizing hand as occupied by that action.

When one or both hands are needed for the current action, relocate, store, or omit unrelated handheld occupational objects. For example, a conductor actively using a baton should retain the other hand for conducting gestures rather than holding an identity-reinforcing document folder.

Default paired wearable items, including ordinary gloves, to a visually coherent pair. Use a single glove or another asymmetric paired item only when a specific action, protection need, fitted device, cultural practice, or visible character circumstance explains:

1. which side carries the item
2. why that side needs it
3. why the opposite side remains different

Otherwise make the pair symmetric or omit it.

For every candidate, establish:

1. What aspect of the character, activity, environment, relationship, or composition produced it?
2. Why is it worn, carried, tied, hung, draped, stored, or held?
3. How is it visibly attached, connected, supported, accessed, or allowed to move?
4. What life trace, identity information, wearing relationship, silhouette change, or compositional value does it add?
5. Does another object already perform the same role?
6. Does its visual weight match its design responsibility?
7. What is its single current state?
8. What supports its weight, secures it, and provides access?
9. Does it conflict with hand occupancy or require unexplained bilateral asymmetry?

Large shoulder, back, handheld, or multi-region objects must be explored as candidates and checked for action and wearing conflicts. Do not reject them during candidate generation merely because they are nonessential or strongly change the silhouette.

## Chest Objects and High-Visual-Weight Ornaments

Chest objects may fasten, close, connect, counterweight, carry, identify, commemorate, express ritual meaning, or act as a focal point. They do not require a practical function, but their source and visual responsibility must be credible.

Treat any large, bright, luminous, precious-looking, or strongly contrasting object at the center chest as a primary visual element. Retain it only when:

- it has a visible and credible attachment, support, or connection structure
- it relates to the garment construction, wearing relationship, equipment, or established character
- its visual weight matches the character and overall style
- it has at least one structural echo and one restrained visual echo elsewhere
- removing it would materially weaken structure, identity readability, or composition

A chest ornament that fastens a cape or harness must visibly connect to straps, chains, brackets, setting claws, reinforced bases, or another plausible load-bearing structure. Show the direction of support rather than floating the ornament on the torso.

Gems, large metal ornaments, ornate brooches, luminous cores, and similar high-visual-weight objects are low-probability candidates, not routine configuration. Consider them only when supported by culture, ceremony, religion, performance, wealth, identity, a clear personal history, equipment function, or a genuine need for a strong focal point. Everyday characters should normally prefer lived-in personal objects and plausible carried items. Minimal or monochrome clothing alone is not sufficient justification.

When a conspicuous ornament is retained, echo it with restraint through garment cut direction, seam or layering structure, hardware shape, limited color repetition, differently scaled secondary details, or related but nonidentical structural relationships. Do not mechanically repeat the same gem, emblem, or pattern across the body.

## Density Control

- **Restrained:** few details and one dominant focal point.
- **Balanced:** several meaningful details with clear hierarchy. Use by default.
- **Rich:** complex decoration with deliberate grouping and negative space.

Before selecting or removing a derived item, check:

1. What aspect of the character produced it?
2. Why is it worn, carried, tied, hung, draped, stored, or held?
3. How is it fixed, connected, accessed, or allowed to move?
4. Does it create a useful wearing relationship, life trace, silhouette change, rhythm, hierarchy, or identity cue?
5. Does another item already perform the same role?
6. Does its visual weight match its design responsibility?
7. What identity information, character specificity, structural value, or compositional value would be lost if it were removed?
8. Does it fulfill a planned element role or merely repeat an existing motif?
9. Does the surrounding region still contain enough quiet space?
10. If it is large or conspicuous, is its structural and character basis strong enough?
11. Is its current state unambiguous?
12. Are its load support, retention, and access path credible?
13. Does it occupy a hand or body location needed by the current action?
14. If it is one half of a normally paired item, is the asymmetry visibly justified?

Omit an item when it lacks a credible source, wearing relationship, or clear visual responsibility; duplicates another object; creates unresolved conflict; has no plausible support or access path; or exists only to satisfy accessory count or region variety. Do not omit it merely because it is nonessential. Accessories should appear with high probability after active derivation, while high-visual-weight ornaments should remain low probability.
