# Professional Visual Language

Use this process for every character with an occupation. Create a game-readable professional outfit without relying on a fixed profession-to-outfit mapping.

## Establish a Real Occupation

Choose a real, specific occupation that can be clearly explained through:

- concrete responsibilities
- normal work environments
- common tools or equipment
- occupational clothing, safety requirements, or identity markers

Replace vague conceptual titles when their real duties cannot be explained. Prefer reality as the identity foundation, not as the final visual limit.

## Generate Player-Readable Visual Sources

Before selecting garments:

1. Identify the broader professional field.
2. Infer the fantasy and first visual associations ordinary players may connect with that field.
3. Generate multiple candidate visual sources from relevant clothing archetypes, uniforms, historical dress, cultural imagery, popular visual language, symbols, colors, tools, procedures, and silhouettes.
4. Compare the candidates against the character's identity, behavior, cultural background, scene, and world.
5. Preserve the strongest sources for the separate core-element derivation step.

Do not automatically select the first or most familiar association. Do not maintain or use a fixed profession-to-outfit mapping. Different characters with the same occupation may select different motifs.

Visual sources are not necessarily deployable design elements. Broad ideas such as `medicine`, `ocean`, `bureaucracy`, `authority`, or `uniform` must be refined into concrete core elements in `core-visual-elements.md` before they organize clothing and accessories.

## Prepare Professional Anchors

- Identify a small number of real occupational tools, protective items, identifiers, procedures, or construction requirements that can clarify the specific occupation.
- Identify which visual sources may influence silhouette, structure, color arrangement, or core-element derivation.
- Preserve professional readability while allowing the later core element and outfit design to become character-specific.
- Let cultural stereotypes improve immediate readability, but do not treat them as the complete design.
- Do not directly copy an existing character, stock costume, or completed uniform design.

Use this approximate emphasis as a design check, not arithmetic:

- 60% player-readable professional visual language
- 25% character-specific game exaggeration
- 15% real occupational equipment and identifiers

## Failure Conditions

Rebuild the design when:

- removing badges, documents, and small tools makes the broad professional field unreadable
- the outfit is mainly generic modern workwear, a generic suit, a generic jacket, or generic tactical clothing
- real equipment carries all occupational readability
- literal workplace accuracy makes the character look like an ordinary worker rather than a game character
- multiple sources compete without a usable direction
- the selected sources ignore the character, culture, scene, or world
- a broad visual source is incorrectly reported as the finished core element

## Reviewable Output

Before `【最终服装设计】`, report:

```text
【职业视觉推导】
现实职业：
职业领域：
玩家第一视觉联想：
候选视觉来源：
优先视觉来源：
现实职业锚点：
角色化改造方向：
```

Keep this section concise and reviewable. Pass the strongest sources into the core-element derivation step. Do not copy this section into the final image prompt; compile only the selected visible results.
